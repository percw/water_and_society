## 24. The Attribution Gap — DiD Cannot Distinguish Water from Alternative Explanations

**The Problem:** Iteration 06 demonstrated that the DiD framework robustly confirms Britain's GDP divergence (~1,100–1,600 2011$ relative to controls) but the temporal permutation test (p=0.728) revealed that the effect is not specific to the NLP-derived treatment year T₀=1758. Any treatment year in 1720–1860 produces a significant DiD. This means the DiD proves Britain diverged, but cannot attribute the divergence specifically to the hydro-social linguistic shift. Coal advocates (Malm 2016), institutionalists (Acemoglu & Robinson 2012), and imperial extraction theorists (Pomeranz 2000) could all claim the same DiD coefficient supports their narrative.

**The Required Remedy:** A DiD framework that uses **water vocabulary intensity itself** as the treatment mechanism, not merely "being Britain after year X." Three strategies:

1. **Continuous-Treatment Intensity DiD** — Replace binary Post with the actual water commodification ratio as continuous treatment intensity. If GDP divergence tracks water intensity specifically, not just time, that pins the mechanism to water.

2. **Placebo Vocabulary Tournament** — Derive T₀ from competing vocabulary categories (religious, military, fossil). If the event-study pattern (clean pre-trends + gradual post-treatment acceleration) is unique to water vocabulary, that eliminates alternative linguistic explanations.

3. **Pre-Steam Mechanism Test (1700-1810)** — Restrict to the era where water was the dominant power technology. Use water vocabulary intensity as treatment with fossil vocabulary as a competing covariate. If water dominates fossil in explaining early divergence, the first-mover hypothesis is validated for the period where it matters.
