# Abstract

The traditional chronology of the British Industrial Revolution credits fossil fuels—specifically coal extraction and the steam engine—as the primary catalysts that liberated human economics from the constraints of nature. This paper refines the institutional "steam-first" timeline by demonstrating that a measurable structural break in Britain's macroeconomic trajectory originated during the earlier era of water infrastructure, preceding and establishing the preconditions for widespread fossil adoption. By applying Natural Language Processing (NLP) to the historical Google Books `eng_gb_2019` corpus, we identify the exact structural shift wherein Britain's printed vocabulary transitioned from viewing water as a natural hazard to commodifying it as an engineered asset (the "Hydro-Social Shift").

Our analysis leverages the 1761 opening of the Bridgewater Canal as a definitive exogenous infrastructure shock. Deploying 1761 as the treatment threshold within a robust Difference-in-Differences (DiD) framework using Maddison Project historical GDP data, we find that Britain's post-shock economic trajectory diverged by an additional $1,251 GDP per capita relative to continental European controls ($p = 0.042$, HAC). We validate the mechanism behind this shock using NLP: we trace the `eng_gb_2019` corpus and identify 1766 as the exact structural crossover wherein Britain's printed vocabulary permanently transitioned from naturalistic to engineered water terminology. Crucially, 47% of Britain's ultimate industrial divergence was established during this pre-steam "Water Era," decades before steam engines achieved commercial dominance. Placebo falsification tournaments across alternative industrial and agricultural vocabularies yield noisy or null effects, demonstrating that only the water infrastructure shock produces a clean event study aligned with GDP divergence. The data positions the canal era not as the sole engine of industrial modernity, but as a necessary precondition—an era of "geographical symbiosis" that created the integrated markets, accumulated capital, and systemic demand which the subsequent fossil transition would amplify.
