**Review Submission: "The Hydro-Social Shift: A Linguistic Difference-in-Differences Analysis of the British Geographic Preconditions for Geometric Growth"**

Dear Editors,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript for consideration. Our paper challenges the established mineral-centric chronology of the British Industrial Revolution by introducing a novel text-as-data methodology. We test the hypothesis that the foundation of Britain's initial macroeconomic takeoff was rooted not in the subversion of nature via fossil extraction, but in "geographical symbiosis"—a cooperative capitalization of natural riverine and topographical endowments.

We innovate methodologically by anchoring our formal Difference-in-Differences (DiD) econometrics on the Maddison Project Database to a strict exogenous historical shock: the 1761 opening of the Bridgewater Canal, which catalyzed Britain's canal mania. To definitively trace the mechanism of this infrastructural shock, we introduce a novel text-as-data methodology. Deploying unsupervised Natural Language Processing (NLP) on the Google Books English corpus, we quantitatively isolated that exactly five years after the canal opening (1766), British print vocabulary experienced a permanent structural semantic crossover—shifting from conceptualizing water as a natural hazard to commodifying it as an engineered asset.

By utilizing the specifically exogenous 1761 physical shock as the $T_0$ treatment variable against French and Dutch controls, we find that the massive canal and water-wheel era accounted for 47% of early British macroeconomic divergence decades before steam technology reached national commercial prominence. 

Addressing recent advances regarding the fragility of historical DiD parallel trends and endogenous identification (Rambachan and Roth 2023; Roth et al. 2023), our use of a discrete historical exogenous shock cleanly bypasses the endogeneity concerns inherent in purely text-derived treatments. Furthermore, we deploy extensive vocabulary-based placebo testing across rival sectors (coal, textiles, finance). These falsification tournaments confirm the unique, highly significant causal alignment of water infrastructure with geometric growth, structurally preceding the fossil revolution.

We believe this interdisciplinary fusion of computational linguistics, robust historical econometrics, and environmental history offers a critical revision to how classical energy transitions are theorized, directly engaging the historiographical frameworks of Pomeranz, Malm, and Tvedt.

The manuscript has not been previously published and is not under consideration elsewhere. We confirm there are no conflicts of interest.
